Work Together

Let's build the thing your AI strategy is quietly missing.

Not a deck. Not a workshop. The actual operational machinery that keeps your AI paying off as the company changes.

How I think about working together

Most consulting engagements are built around a framework. The consultant has a framework. The client has a problem. The consultant applies the framework to the problem and produces a report. Everyone shakes hands.

I don't work like that.

The reason I've been useful across knowledge management, financial regulation, and now AI governance is that the work isn't about applying a template. It's about listening carefully to a specific organization, understanding what it's actually trying to become, and then building — with the people who'll have to live with it — the operational scaffolding that makes that transformation possible.

So the engagements below are less "products I sell" and more "shapes these collaborations tend to take." The real thing happens when we talk and figure out what you actually need.

Five shapes an engagement tends to take. Your situation might look like one of these — or it might look like something we need to invent together.

The reckoning

You know your AI governance is not where it needs to be. You don't know exactly how bad it is. We start there — with a clear-eyed look at what you've actually deployed, what's coming for you regulatorily, and what the gap is between those two things. No judgment, no panic. Just a map you can actually act on.

The scaffolding

You've done the reckoning — or you've already got the urgency — and now you need to actually build. Governance structures, policies, risk classification, documentation architecture, human oversight mechanisms, audit trails. The invisible infrastructure that turns good intentions into defensible operations.

8–12 weeks

The translation

You're a fintech or financial services company with real regulatory operations already in place. You need those same muscles pointed at AI. This is the engagement where I do what I do best: take a compliance operation that works, and extend it into a domain the company hasn't governed before without breaking what already works.

The second opinion

You've got a plan. Your legal team signed off. Your technology team signed off. Something's still bothering you. This is the engagement where I pressure-test what you've built — from the operations side, the side that has to actually run it when you're not in the room. Sometimes I find nothing and you sleep better. Sometimes I find the thing.

The standing conversation

You want someone in your corner as you navigate AI governance over time. Not a full-time hire. Not a consultant for a defined project. Someone who knows your situation, who you can call when something comes up, who reads the regulatory landscape so you don't have to. Monthly cadence, ad-hoc availability.

The conversations that tend to end up with us working together

The CEO

"We've bet a lot on AI. I don't think we can explain — to a regulator or to our board — what our governance actually is. That scares me a little."

The Chief Risk Officer

"My team understands financial risk. I don't think they understand AI risk. And I can feel us getting exposed on a flank we haven't learned to defend yet."

The Head of Compliance

"We've spent a decade building the KYC program. I can see the AI regulations coming. Nobody on my team has the bandwidth or the experience to build that same infrastructure all over again for a different domain."

The Head of Product

"Our engineers are shipping AI features faster than our governance can keep up. I need someone who can build the operational layer around what we're shipping, without slowing down the shipping."

What happens after you get in touch

Short version: we talk, I tell you honestly whether I can help, and if we both want to keep going, we figure out what that looks like. Here's the longer version.

01

A real conversation, not a sales call

Thirty minutes. You tell me what's actually keeping you up at night. I ask questions. I'm trying to understand your situation, not pitch you. If at the end of the call I don't think I'm the right person, I'll tell you — and probably recommend someone else.

02

A proposal you can actually read

If we're going forward, I write you a proposal that fits on two pages. Scope, deliverables, timeline, investment, assumptions. No consultant-speak. No hidden change orders. If you have questions, we get on a second call and I change the proposal.

03

The work, with you — not at you

I don't disappear for six weeks and come back with a deck. I work alongside your teams, with weekly checkpoints, with drafts you see as they develop. The best deliverables are the ones your own people helped shape — because those are the ones that actually get used after I'm gone.

04

A handoff, not a dependency

My goal isn't to become indispensable. It's to leave you with something your team owns and can run without me. Documentation, training, a transition plan. If we set it up right, the last conversation we have is short — because nothing is on fire.

The first call is free and worth it either way.

Even if we don't end up working together, you'll leave the conversation with a sharper view of what you're dealing with. Worst case, you've had a thirty-minute chat with someone who's thought about this for a long time. Best case, we figure out how to solve something that's been bothering you.
