Everyone is racing into AI. Almost no one is building the thing that makes it pay off.

They forget what they know. They skip the measurements. They abandon the safety rails. Like amateurs sprinting toward a cliff with a pitch deck.

I build the invisible scaffolding that turns AI from a bet into a business. The processes, the guardrails, the mechanisms that decide whether your investment compounds or evaporates. I've done it for financial regulation across nine jurisdictions. The work now is the same work — with stakes that are higher and deadlines that are closer.

How I got here

I thought I'd missed the train.

A few months ago I had some unexpected time on my hands, and I did the thing you're not supposed to do at forty-four: I looked up from the work and asked where it was actually taking me.

Fifteen years in knowledge management. Then a bold detour into fintech operations, starting from scratch, learning fast, doing well. I built KYC frameworks for a company operating across nine jurisdictions and millions of customers. By most measures, I was succeeding.

But when I looked up, something felt wrong. The market was turning. The conversations I was sitting in were about things I'd spent a decade learning — and the new conversations, the ones getting the oxygen and the capital, were about something else entirely. I started doing that quiet calculation people do in their forties about the doors that might have closed behind them.

Then something shifted. I realized the train I thought I'd missed hadn't actually left yet.

I'd been looking at AI the way most people look at AI — as a technology problem, a thing for engineers and researchers. But the more I paid attention, the more I saw what nobody wanted to say out loud: the AI rush has almost no operational spine. Companies are pouring millions into models and platforms while forgetting the things that make any business actually work. Measurement. Governance. Safety. The boring disciplines that turn a bet into a business.

That's not a technology gap. That's an operations gap. And operations is what I do.

· · ·

The people building AI right now are not amateurs. They are brilliant, ambitious, moving fast. But they are acting like amateurs, because amateurs are what you become when you abandon every discipline you've ever learned in the name of not being left behind.

I'm not trying to slow anyone down. I'm trying to make sure that when the dust settles, what you've built is still standing.

The thing nobody is saying

The AI conversation is being held by technologists and lawyers. The operators are missing from the room.

Risk classification. Human oversight. Audit trails. Escalation paths. Change management. Measurement frameworks. Every one of these is a solved problem in regulated industries. We've been doing them for decades. They just need to be translated.

The EU AI Act takes full effect in August 2026. Regulators in every major market are following. And the companies that treat this as a paperwork exercise — something to delegate to legal and forget about — are going to discover, the hard way, that governance without operations is just a document nobody reads.

The companies that win the AI era won't be the ones who move fastest. They'll be the ones who can still move — with their licenses intact, their customers' trust earned, and their systems holding together — five years from now.

What this looks like in practice

Four questions I help organizations answer.

Not frameworks. Not methodologies. Questions with consequences — the kind you can't afford to get wrong, and can't afford to ignore any longer.

01

Which of your AI systems are actually high-risk — and do you know what that means for you?

Most companies can't produce a clean inventory of the AI touching their business, let alone a classification against the regulations coming for them. This is where the work starts. Not with the regulation. With knowing what you've got.

02

When your AI makes a decision a regulator asks about, can you show your work?

Documentation, audit trails, decision logs, the chain of accountability when something goes sideways. The boring stuff that turns into the expensive stuff the moment an auditor walks in and you don't have it.

03

How do you know your AI is still doing what you hired it to do?

Models drift. Data shifts. The thing that worked beautifully in Q1 is quietly failing in Q3 and nobody notices until a customer complaint or a regulator's letter. Ongoing monitoring isn't optional — it's the only thing standing between you and surprise.

04

When the company changes — and it will — can your AI governance change with it?

The worst governance systems are rigid. They're built for the company you were when you hired the consultant, not the company you become. The real work is building something that flexes as you grow, pivots when you pivot, and doesn't need to be torn down and rebuilt every time the strategy shifts.
