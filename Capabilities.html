The Work

The boring work that saves the company.

When AI fails, it rarely fails in the model. It fails in the part of the business that nobody was paying attention to — the operational layer underneath.

What I actually do

The simplest way to describe it is this: I build the mechanism that turns AI from an expense into a return.

That sounds abstract, so let me be specific. When a company deploys AI at scale, three things quietly determine whether the investment pays off — and all three are operational, not technical.

The first is measurement. Most companies can tell you how much they spent on AI last quarter. Almost none can tell you what they got back. Not in a defensible, documented, auditable way. The models run, the outputs flow, the dashboards look busy — and the actual question of whether the thing is working, by what definition, for whom, gets lost in the noise.

The second is safety. Not the philosophical kind. The practical kind. What happens when the model gets it wrong? Who notices? How fast? What's the process for pulling the cord? Who has the authority to pull it? Most AI deployments today have no answer. The model fails silently, someone eventually complains, and by the time anyone's paying attention, the damage is done.

The third is adaptability. Companies change. Strategies shift. Markets move. But the AI systems you deploy today are calibrated to the company you are today. When you pivot — and you will — does your AI pivot with you, or does it quietly become a drag on the thing you're trying to become?

These aren't exotic problems. They are the same problems banks and financial institutions have been solving for a century. The only thing new is the technology. The discipline required is old.

My job is to bring that discipline to AI. To translate the regulatory operations playbook I've been running for more than a decade into a domain that desperately needs it but doesn't yet know how to ask for it.

The same work, with new stakes

For the past four years, I've been doing this inside a global fintech, for financial regulation. Nine jurisdictions. Hundreds of operational people. Millions of customers. The regulators — in the US, Europe, Asia — demand not just that you comply with the rules but that you can prove you comply, document it, monitor it, audit it, and show the work.

That work is what I'm now bringing to AI. The EU AI Act is coming in August 2026. NIST has published its framework in the US. ISO 42001 is moving through certification. Every sophisticated regulator in the world is arriving at roughly the same conclusion: AI needs the same operational discipline we've always required of anything that makes consequential decisions about people's money, livelihood, or rights.

Most companies are not ready. Not because they're careless — because they're moving fast, and because nobody has told them that the operational layer is the difference between winning and getting expensively corrected.

I'm here to tell them.

The work I'm bringing to AI is the work I was already doing. Here's the shape of it.

People ask how KYC has anything to do with AI. They are almost the same job.

In financial regulation

You classify customers by risk tier. You apply proportional controls. You document every decision so you can show the work to a regulator five years later. You build monitoring to catch drift before it becomes a scandal. You run human oversight on the edge cases the machine can't handle. You update the whole system when the law changes.
